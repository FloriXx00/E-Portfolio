Computer Science Proof Lifts Limits on Quantum Entanglement

Mordechai Rorvig

Definitions

  • Term 1: NLTS (No Low-Energy Trivial States) conjecture A theoretical statement in quantum complexity theory. It claims that some quantum systems remain highly entangled even in their low-energy (almost ground state) configurations. This means they cannot be approximated by "trivial" (unentangled or weakly entangled) quantum states, suggesting inherent computational hardness.
  • Term 2: PCP (Probabilistically Checkable Proof) theoremA major result in classical computational complexity. It states that some mathematical proofs can be verified by checking only a small portion of the proof randomly, with high probability. This implies that approximating certain problems is as hard as solving them exactly. The quantum version of this theorem remains unproven, but the NLTS result is a step toward it.
  • Term 3: Entanglement (splątanie kwantowe) A uniquely quantum correlation between particles, where the state of one particle is instantly linked to the state of another, no matter the distance. Entanglement is a key resource in quantum computation and quantum information.
  • Term 4: Low-energy state (stan niskoenergetyczny) A configuration of a quantum system that is close to its ground state (minimum possible energy). In classical systems, such states are often simple. In quantum systems, they can still carry complex entanglement structures.
  • Term 5: Quantum PCP conjecture An open problem in quantum complexity theory. It posits that verifying solutions to certain quantum problems is still hard, even if we only care about approximate (low-energy) solutions — analogous to the classical PCP theorem.

Summary

This article discusses a significant advancement in quantum computational complexity: the proof of the NLTS (No Low-Energy Trivial States) conjecture. This conjecture posits that certain quantum systems remain computationally complex even in their low-energy states, implying that their entanglement cannot be easily simplified or approximated. The authors illustrate this concept using a thought experiment: imagine placing numerous bar magnets in a bathtub. Each magnet interacts with its neighbors, seeking an optimal orientation. Predicting the final arrangement becomes exceedingly complex as the number of magnets increases. When these magnets are replaced with quantum particles, the complexity escalates further due to quantum entanglement. Previously, the PCP (Probabilistically Checkable Proof) theorem in classical computer science established that certain problems remain hard to approximate. The quantum analogue, the quantum PCP conjecture, remains unproven. However, the recent proof of the NLTS conjecture serves as a crucial step toward this goal. It demonstrates that even low-energy states of specific quantum systems cannot be approximated by simple, unentangled states. This finding has profound implications for quantum computing and our understanding of quantum systems. It suggests that certain quantum states inherently possess complex entanglement structures, making them resistant to simplification. This resilience is essential for developing robust quantum error correction methods and understanding the fundamental limits of quantum computation.

The Statistical Building Blocks of Animal Movement Simulations

  • Term 1: Statistical Movement Elements (StaMEs) Short segments of animal movement data (e.g., 10–30 GPS points), characterized by summary statistics like mean speed and turning angle. They act as “building blocks” for modeling behavior.

Summary

This article introduces a novel framework for understanding and simulating animal movement, based on extracting statistical patterns from relocation data. These segments are derived directly from real or simulated GPS data and serve as the fundamental building blocks for analyzing complex movement behavior. The authors argue that traditional models of animal movement often rely on abstract or oversimplified assumptions. This bottom-up segmentation strategy enables ecologists to study movement in a way that reflects natural behavior more accurately. It also allows researchers to simulate how an individual might respond to changing conditions, such as encountering a barrier, searching for food, or adapting to a new environment. The model’s flexibility supports the generation of hypotheses about animal behavior that can be tested both in simulations and in field observations. The study concludes that StaMEs offer a practical and scalable tool for organizing animal movement data into interpretable units. By capturing the fine-scale structure of movement and linking it to environmental and internal variables, the approach has strong potential for applications in conservation, behavioral ecology, and even disease modeling.]

Machine Learning for Modeling Animal Movement

Dhanushi A. Wijeyakulasuriya, Elizabeth W. Eisenhauer, Ben- jamin A. Shaby, Ephraim M. Hanks

Definitions

  • Term 1: Random Forest An ensemble machine learning method that builds multiple decision trees during training and averages their results. Used for classification and regression, it is robust to overfitting and handles high-dimensional data well.
  • Term 2: Neural Network A computational model inspired by the brain, composed of layers of interconnected units (neurons). Neural networks learn patterns from data and are used for both classification and prediction tasks.
  • Term 3: Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) A type of neural network designed for sequence data. It has memory, meaning that past inputs influence current outputs. Commonly used in time-series modeling, including animal movement over time
  • Term 4: Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) An advanced form of RNN that solves the problem of forgetting long-term dependencies. It uses internal gates to control what to keep and what to forget, making it effective for modeling long sequences, such as migration paths.
  • Term 5: Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE) Model A parametric model that describes random processes evolving over time. Used to simulate smooth, continuous animal paths based on environmental gradients and movement tendencies.
  • Term 6: Motility Surface A map that describes how fast an animal is likely to move at each location. High motility areas promote faster movement, while low motility areas slow animals down. It helps model movement intensity in space.
  • Term 7: Potential SurfaceA spatial surface that represents areas of attraction or repulsion, influenc- ing the direction of movement. Animals tend to move down the potential gradient, similar to how objects roll downhill.
  • Term 8: Behavioral State A label describing the type of movement an animal is performing at a given time (e.g., foraging, migrating, resting). In the model, each state has its own characteristic movement pattern.
  • Term 9: Velocity Prediction The process of estimating the animal’s speed and direction of movement, often based on past movement and environmental variables. It’s the second step in the two-part modeling framework.
  • Term 10: One-Step Ahead Prediction A type of prediction where the model estimates the next position or behavior of the animal based only on the most recent data. Used to assess short-term accuracy of models.
  • Term 11: Simulation Generating artificial movement data using a trained model. Simulations help test how well models reproduce real-world behaviors over longer periods and under various scenarios.

Summary

This article presents a flexible and general framework for predicting animal movement using machine learning and deep learning techniques. The authors propose a two-step model: first, classify the behavioral state of the animal, and second, predict the animal's velocity based on that state. Unlike traditional parametric models that rely on fixed assumptions about animal behavior, this data-driven approach uses flexible nonparametric methods to improve prediction and simulation quality. The study focuses on two data sets: high-resolution 2D movement data from an entire colony of carpenter ants, and long-range migratory data from lesser black-backed gulls. For the ants, the authors model both individual and collective behavior using Random Forests, feedforward and recurrent neural networks, and a baseline stochastic differential equation (SDE) model. They show that machine learning models—especially LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) neural networks—perform best for predicting short-term movement (one step ahead), while the SDE model better captures long-range patterns. To represent different behaviors, the authors define three states for ants: stationary, moving inside the nest, and outside the nest. For gulls, four states are defined: northern range, southbound migration, southern range, and northbound migration. Simulations over 1000 time steps show that while no model perfectly replicates all movement dynamics, the LSTM model comes closest to reproducing realistic individual trajectories. The authors also explore model performance for simulated gull migration, showing that LSTM again outperforms Random Forests in capturing state transitions and directional patterns. One advantage of the machine learning approach is its generalizability across species. However, a key limitation is interpretability: while parametric models yield clearer insights into behavior and environment interactions, machine learning methods offer higher predictive power but less clarity. In conclusion, the study provides strong evidence that machine learning and deep learning models can effectively model and simulate animal movement, especially when high-resolution data is available. Their general framework can be adapted to various ecological applications, including disease modeling, conservation planning, and behavioral ecology.

Article 11: Hybrid moiré excitons and trions in twisted $\mathrm{MoTe_2}$–$\mathrm{MoSe_2}$ heterobilayers

Shen Zhao, Xin Huang, Roland Gillen, Zhijie Li, Song Liu, Kenji Watanabe, Takashi Taniguchi, Janina Maultzsch, James Hone, Alexander Högele, Anvar S. Baimuratov

Definitions

  • Term 1: Moiré superlattice A larger-scale periodic structure formed when two atomic layers are overlaid with a twist or slight lattice mismatch. It creates a new periodic potential modifying electrons, holes, and excitons. In this article, the $\mathrm{MoTe_2}$–$\mathrm{MoSe_2}$ heterobilayers form a moiré pattern controlled by the twist angle.
  • Term 2: Continuum model A theoretical approach in which the atomic-scale lattice is replaced by a smoothly varying effective potential. It captures long-wavelength physics of twisted heterobilayers, e.g. resonant interlayer tunneling and moiré potentials.
  • Term 3: Resonant interlayer tunneling A process in which electrons or holes coherently tunnel between the two layers when their energy levels are nearly matched. It hybridizes exciton states of different layers, enhancing optical activity.
  • Term 4: Oscillator strength A measure of the probability of optical transitions (absorption or emission). Higher oscillator strength implies stronger light–matter coupling. In the hybrid exciton context, it indicates how bright the exciton appears in optical spectra.
  • Term 5: Landé g-factor A dimensionless parameter describing the splitting of energy levels in a magnetic field. In the paper, g-factors of moiré excitons vary with twist angle, reflecting their spin and valley structure.

Summary

This article investigates the optical and electronic properties of hybrid moiré excitons and trions in twisted molybdenum telluride–molybdenum selenide heterobilayers. These are bilayers of two different transition metal dichalcogenides, misaligned by a small twist angle, leading to the formation of a moiré superlattice. This larger-scale periodic structure creates a new effective potential, significantly altering the behavior of excitons—bound states of electrons and holes. Using a continuum model, the authors describe how interlayer interactions affect the energy levels and optical activity of these quasiparticles. A key phenomenon discussed is resonant interlayer tunneling, where carriers such as electrons can coherently tunnel between layers when their energy states are aligned. This process leads to hybridization of exciton states from both layers, forming new optically active states that would otherwise be dark in untwisted or aligned systems. The oscillator strength of these hybrid excitons is shown to depend strongly on the twist angle, meaning their coupling to light can be enhanced or suppressed based on geometric alignment. The paper also analyzes the magnetic response of these excitons using the Landé $g$-factor, which quantifies how their energy levels split under an external magnetic field. The measured values of the $g$-factors vary with the twist angle, providing further evidence of the exciton's hybridized nature and the moiré potential's influence. Overall, the study demonstrates how carefully engineered moiré heterostructures allow for tunable excitonic properties. By controlling the twist angle and using advanced theoretical tools like continuum models, it is possible to access and manipulate exotic quasiparticles for future applications in quantum optics and valleytronics.

Emerging Exciton Physics in Transition Metal Dichalcogenide Heterobilayers

Emma C. Regan, Danqing Wang, Eunice Y. Paik, Yongxin Zeng, Long Zhang, Jihang Zhu, Allan H. MacDonald, Hui Deng, Feng Wang

Definitions

  • Term 1: Moiré mini Brillouin zone (mBZ) A smaller, folded Brillouin zone in reciprocal space that arises from the large real-space periodicity of a moiré superlattice. Exciton bands fold into the mBZ, potentially becoming flat or opening gaps
  • Term 2: Exciton localization The confinement of excitons to specific potential minima within the moiré superlattice. It occurs when the moiré potential depth exceeds exciton kinetic energy, leading to site-specific exciton trapping
  • Term 3: Exciton-polariton A hybrid quasiparticle formed by strong coupling between an exciton and a photon confined in an optical cavity. These emerge in TMD heterostructures placed inside cavities
  • Term 4: Flatband An energy band with very low dispersion (narrow width), where particles have nearly zero group velocity. Flatbands enhance correlations and can support exotic collective states
  • Term 5: Berry curvature A momentum-space field describing the geometrical properties of Bloch bands. Non-zero Berry curvature in exciton bands (within mBZ) can lead to topological effects

Summary

This article reviews the rapidly developing field of exciton physics in twisted transition metal dichalcogenide (TMD) heterobilayers. When two monolayers such as $\mathrm{MoSe_2}$ and $\mathrm{WSe_2}$ are stacked with a small twist angle, they form a moiré superlattice that dramatically alters the electronic and optical properties of the system. One key effect is the formation of a moiré mini Brillouin zone (mBZ), a reduced version of the reciprocal lattice due to the enlarged moiré unit cell. In this new zone, exciton bands can flatten or fold, creating narrow bandwidths—called flatbands—where particle motion is suppressed. These flatbands enhance Coulomb interactions, enabling the emergence of exotic collective states. Another important phenomenon is exciton localization. If the moiré potential is deep enough, excitons become trapped in specific spatial regions, forming arrays of quantum-dot-like sites. This localization gives rise to discrete energy levels and strongly modifies the photoluminescence spectrum. Moreover, when these heterobilayers are placed inside optical cavities, they can support exciton-polaritons—quasiparticles resulting from the strong coupling between excitons and confined photons. These hybrid modes inherit properties from both light and matter, allowing for tunable quantum-optical behavior. The article also emphasizes the role of Berry curvature in exciton bands. In systems with broken inversion symmetry, non-zero Berry curvature arises in the mBZ, giving rise to topological effects such as valley-selective optical transitions or excitonic Hall currents. These geometrical properties are not just theoretical curiosities—they have direct experimental signatures and technological implications. In summary, twisted TMD heterobilayers provide an ideal platform to study strongly correlated, topologically rich exciton systems. The combination of flatbands, localization, hybrid light–matter coupling, and Berry curvature effects makes these systems highly tunable and relevant for quantum simulation and optoelectronics.

Definitions

  • Term 1: Wannier obstruction The inability to construct exponentially localized Wannier functions for certain topological bands. The article notes that no Wannier obstruction exists here, allowing for accurate tight-binding modeling
  • Term 2: Orbital interference Quantum interference between electron hopping paths via different orbitals (e.g., from honeycomb vs triangular sites). This mechanism explains the emergence of magic angles and band structure features
  • Term 3: Chern number A topological invariant characterizing energy bands; the article tracks how Chern numbers of moiré bands evolve with twist angle, indicating transitions between Chern insulator states
  • Term 4: Magic angle A special twist angle where destructive interference in hopping leads to nearly flat (dispersionless) bands. At these angles, strongly correlated physics like flat-band phenomena arise
  • Term 5: van Hove singularityA peak (divergence) in the electronic density of states caused by saddle points in the band structure. The model reproduces both Chern ferromagnetic and van Hove–driven antiferromagnetic phases

The goal is to bridge the gap between small-angle and large-angle regimes, capturing both orbital effects and topological features in a single framework. One of the key contributions of the model is that it avoids any Wannier obstruction. This means it is possible to construct well-localized Wannier functions for the relevant bands, enabling accurate modeling of electronic behavior using tight-binding methods even in topological regimes. A central mechanism explored in the article is orbital interference. In summary, the article presents a flexible and physically motivated tight-binding framework that captures both the geometric and topological richness of twisted TMD bilayers. Week 15

Non Equilibrium Green’s Functions for Dummies: Introduction to the One Particle NEGF equations

Magnus Paulsson

Definitions

  • Term 1: Retarded/Advanced Green’s function Types of Green’s functions distinguishing causal (retarded) and time-reversed (advanced) responses. Retarded ($G$) describes propagation forward in time, advanced ($G^{\dagger}$) backward
  • Term 2: Self-energy A complex function, $\Sigma = \tau g \tau^\dagger$, that incorporates the effect of contacts (leads) on the device region in the discrete Schrödinger equation. It modifies the effective Hamiltonian used to obtain $G$
  • Term 3: Spectral function Denoted $A = i(G - G^{\dagger})$, it captures the system's density of states and the full set of energy eigenstates. It directly relates to physical observables like the charge density
  • Term 4: Transmission function Defined as $T(E) = \text{Tr}( \Gamma_L G \Gamma_R G^{\dagger})$, where $\Gamma_{\{L,R\}} = i(\Sigma_{\{L,R\}} - \Sigma_{\{L,R\}}^{\dagger})$. It quantifies the probability for electrons to transmit through the device
  • Term 5: Continuity equation approach A method to derive current operators via the time-dependent Schrödinger or continuity equation, rather than using a velocity operator, particularly important in discrete tight-binding systems

Summary

This article introduces the core ideas behind the Non-Equilibrium Green’s Function (NEGF) formalism, which is widely used to describe quantum transport in nanoscale systems such as molecular junctions and quantum dots. At the heart of the method are the **retarded** and **advanced Green’s functions**, denoted $G$ and $G^{\dagger}$. These functions describe how an electron propagates through a quantum system either forward or backward in time. Importantly, they encode how the system responds to external perturbations. A key ingredient in this approach is the **self-energy**, $\Sigma$, which accounts for the influence of the leads (electrodes) connected to the central device region. The self-energy modifies the effective Hamiltonian, allowing the calculation of $G$ to include the coupling to the surrounding reservoirs. From $G$ and $\Sigma$, we can define the **spectral function**, $A = i(G - G^{\dagger})$, which reflects the density of available states in the system. This function connects directly to physical observables such as the local density of states and charge density. Perhaps the most practical output of the NEGF method is the **transmission function**, $T(E)$, which tells us the energy-dependent probability that an electron will transmit from one lead to another through the device. It is computed from the Green’s functions and the so-called broadening matrices $\Gamma_L$ and $\Gamma_R$, which are derived from the self-energies. Finally, the article emphasizes the **continuity equation approach** to defining current operators. Instead of using a velocity operator, which can be ambiguous in tight-binding models, the author uses the continuity equation to rigorously derive expressions for the current. This makes the NEGF formalism more consistent and physically transparent, especially for discrete systems. In summary, this tutorial offers a clear and intuitive roadmap to the NEGF formalism, breaking down complex quantum transport concepts into manageable equations and physical interpretations. It is especially valuable for newcomers aiming to understand how quantum currents flow through nanoscale devices.
